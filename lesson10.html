1. What Is HIV? What Is AIDS?

HIV stands for Human Immunodeficiency Virus. It attacks the body's immune system — specifically the CD4 (T) cells that help fight off infections.

AIDS stands for Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome. It is the most advanced stage of HIV, where the immune system is so damaged that the body can't fight off even simple infections (called "opportunistic infections").

Key facts:
  • HIV is a virus — it prevents the body from fighting off infections
  • A person can have HIV for many years without developing symptoms or knowing they're infected
  • There is no cure for HIV, but medication (ART) can control it
  • Estimated 1.1 million people in the US are living with HIV; about 39,500 new cases each year

2. How HIV Is Transmitted

HIV is transmitted through specific body fluids:

  • Blood
  • Semen (and pre-seminal fluid)
  • Vaginal fluids
  • Breast milk
  • Rectal fluids

Common ways HIV is transmitted:

  • Unprotected vaginal or anal sex with an infected person
  • Sharing needles or drug equipment
  • Mother to child during pregnancy, childbirth, or breastfeeding
  • Through oral sex (rare but possible, especially with open sores)
  • Blood transfusions (extremely rare now due to testing)

3. How HIV Is NOT Transmitted

You CANNOT get HIV from:

  • Hugging, shaking hands, or holding hands
  • Sharing food, drinks, or utensils
  • Using the same bathroom or toilet
  • Mosquito bites or other insect bites
  • Dog bites
  • Kissing (closed-mouth; deep kissing has theoretical but extremely low risk)
  • Swimming in the same pool
  • Sharing a toothbrush (not recommended, but not an HIV risk)
  • Through sweat or tears
  • Going to the dentist or doctor (standard precautions prevent this)
Remember: HIV is actually a fragile virus — it dies very quickly outside the body. Casual contact is completely safe.

4. Treatment: Antiretroviral Therapy (ART)

While there is no cure for HIV, Antiretroviral Therapy (ART) has transformed it from a death sentence to a manageable chronic condition:

  • ART uses a combination of medications taken daily
  • The goal is to keep the viral load low (the amount of HIV in the blood)
  • With successful treatment, a person can live a long, healthy life
  • When viral load is "undetectable," the risk of transmitting HIV is effectively zero
  • ART must be taken consistently for life — stopping can cause the virus to become resistant
Universal Precautions: These are safety steps everyone should take to avoid contact with blood and body fluids. They include wearing gloves when giving first aid, not sharing razors or needles, and properly cleaning up blood spills.
✚ A Note for Our Family:

People living with HIV deserve our compassion, not judgment. Many people contracted HIV through no fault of their own — through blood transfusions, from an infected partner, or at birth. Jesus showed love to the sick, the outcast, and the marginalized. We are called to do the same.

"He heals the brokenhearted and binds up their wounds." — Psalm 147:3

5. Red Light / Green Light Activity

This activity classifies behaviors by HIV risk level:

GREEN (No Risk)

  • Go to the movies
  • Work on a project together
  • Hold hands
  • Kiss (closed-mouth)
  • Hug
  • Wrestling
  • Use the same bathroom
  • Go to the dentist
  • Drink from the same glass
  • Share a straw
  • Share lipstick/chapstick
  • Dog bite
  • Mosquito bite
  • Donate blood
  • Give first aid (with gloves)
  • Being on ART (reduces risk)
  • Sexual monogamy (one partner)

RED (High Risk)

  • Vaginal sex (unprotected)
  • Anal sex (unprotected)
  • Oral sex (lower risk but still possible)
  • Share a needle
  • Share injection drug equipment
  • Blood ritual
  • Share a razor
  • Bloody fight
  • Body piercing (unsterile)
  • Getting a tattoo (unsterile)
  • Pierce ears (unsterile equipment)
  • Being bitten by infected person (with blood exchange)
YELLOW (Caution) behaviors have the potential to lead to higher risk: using alcohol or drugs (impairs judgment), deep kissing (if open sores), being together at home alone (can lead to risky decisions), sex with birth control pills only (no STI protection), using spermicide to prevent pregnancy (no STI protection).

6. Myths, Stigma, and Compassion

There are harmful myths and stereotypes about HIV/AIDS:

  • Myth: Only certain groups get HIV. Fact: Anyone can contract HIV regardless of age, gender, race, or orientation.
  • Myth: You can tell if someone has HIV by looking at them. Fact: Many people with HIV look and feel perfectly healthy.
  • Myth: It's always the person's fault. Fact: Many people contracted HIV through circumstances beyond their control.
✚ A Note for Our Family:

Being infected with HIV does not mean someone did something wrong. Babies are born with it. People get it from blood transfusions. Spouses get it from unfaithful partners. Even in cases involving personal choices, our role as Christians is to love, not to judge. Jesus touched the lepers when everyone else avoided them.

"Do not judge, and you will not be judged. Do not condemn, and you will not be condemned. Forgive, and you will be forgiven." — Luke 6:37

7. Key Terms

HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus): A virus that attacks the immune system's CD4 cells, making it harder to fight infections.
AIDS (Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome): The most advanced stage of HIV infection, where the immune system is severely damaged.
ART (Antiretroviral Therapy): Daily medication that keeps HIV viral load low, allowing people to live long, healthy lives.
Viral Load: The amount of HIV in a person's blood. The goal of treatment is to make this "undetectable."
Universal Precautions: Safety measures to avoid contact with blood and body fluids (wearing gloves, not sharing needles, etc.).
Opportunistic Infections: Infections that take advantage of a weakened immune system (common in AIDS).

8. Worksheet Answers (Lesson Wrap-Up)

Lesson 10 (HIV/AIDS Epidemic) — True or False

Statement Answer Why?
The Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV) prevents the body from being able to fight off infections. TRUE HIV attacks the CD4 (T) cells of the immune system. Without these cells, the body cannot effectively fight off infections and diseases.
Body fluids which can spread HIV include blood, semen, vaginal fluids, and urine. FALSE Blood, semen, vaginal fluids, breast milk, and rectal fluids can spread HIV. However, urine cannot spread HIV. Neither can sweat, tears, or saliva.
The goal of antiretroviral therapy (ART) is to extend the infected person's life by keeping his or her HIV viral load low. TRUE ART works by keeping the viral load as low as possible (ideally "undetectable"). This protects the immune system and allows the person to live a long, healthy life.

Lesson 10 (Recognizing and Reducing Risks) — True or False

Statement Answer Why?
Sexual contact is a low-risk activity when it comes to transmitting HIV. FALSE Sexual contact (especially unprotected vaginal and anal sex) is one of the highest-risk activities for HIV transmission.
Universal Precautions help protect a person from coming into contact with someone else's blood and body fluids. TRUE Universal Precautions (like wearing gloves, not sharing needles) are designed to prevent contact with blood and body fluids that could transmit HIV and other infections.
Worn packaging, oils, and storage at high temperatures can interfere with the effectiveness of latex condoms. TRUE Condoms can be damaged by heat, oils (like petroleum jelly), and worn/expired packaging. Always check the expiration date and store condoms properly.

What was the most important thing you learned?

"I learned that HIV attacks the immune system and that there is no cure, but ART medication can help people live long, healthy lives. I also learned the difference between behaviors that are risky for HIV (like sharing needles and unprotected sex) and behaviors that are safe (like hugging, shaking hands, and sharing food). Most importantly, I learned that we should show compassion to people living with HIV, not judgment."
