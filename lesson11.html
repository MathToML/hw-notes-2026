1. Understanding Peer Pressure

Peer pressure is when people your age try to influence your decisions. It can be positive or negative:

Positive pressure: "Stay in school," "Don't use drugs," "Avoid an unplanned pregnancy" — these are all examples of good pressure that encourages healthy choices.

Negative pressure pushes you toward risky or harmful choices:

  • "Everyone's doing it" (they're not)
  • "If you really loved me, you would..." (love doesn't use guilt)
  • "Don't be scared" or "Don't be a baby"
  • "No one will find out" (someone always finds out)
  • "Just this once won't hurt" (famous last words)
Important: Using drugs or alcohol can prevent you from making safe sexual decisions. When you're under the influence, your judgment is impaired and you're more likely to do things you'd never do sober.

2. Three Types of Responses

Passive Response

Going along with what others want even though you don't agree. You don't express your feelings. This can lead to regret and being taken advantage of.

Aggressive Response

Being hostile, threatening, or mean. This escalates conflict and damages relationships.

Assertive Response (THE GOAL)

Expressing your feelings and standing your ground firmly but respectfully. This is the healthiest way to respond to pressure.

Assertive communication includes:
  • Using clear, firm language ("No, I don't want to do that.")
  • Making eye contact
  • Standing up straight (confident body language)
  • Stating your limits and expectations firmly
  • Walking away if the person doesn't respect your answer
✚ A Note for Our Family:

Standing firm in your convictions takes courage, but God promises to give us strength when we need it. You don't have to be rude to be strong — you just have to be clear and confident in what you believe.

"Be strong and courageous. Do not be afraid; do not be discouraged, for the Lord your God will be with you wherever you go." — Joshua 1:9

3. Responding to Pressure Lines

The worksheet gives you practice responding to common pressure lines. Here are sample assertive responses:

Pressure: "Come with me to this great party. There are not going to be any adults and one of the guys is bringing some alcohol."
Response: "No thanks, that sounds risky. I'm not interested in parties without adults or with alcohol. Let's do something else instead — want to come over and play video games?"
Pressure: "If you were really my friend, you would go with me."
Response: "A real friend wouldn't pressure me to do something I'm not comfortable with. I am your friend, and that's exactly why I'm saying no — I don't want either of us to get in trouble."
Pressure: "I won't be your friend anymore."
Response: "If our friendship depends on me doing something dangerous, then maybe we need to rethink this friendship. A real friend respects when I say no."
Pressure: "You will be totally safe with me whatever you do."
Response: "Nobody can guarantee that. I've made up my mind about what I'm comfortable with, and I'm sticking to it. Please respect my decision."
Pressure: "No one will know."
Response: "I would know. And that's what matters. My decision isn't based on whether someone finds out — it's based on what I believe is right."
Pressure: "Come on, we can have a really great time."
Response: "There are plenty of ways to have a great time that don't involve risks. I said no, and I mean it. Let's go do something else."

4. My Escape Plan

Sometimes you need a plan to physically leave a bad situation. The "My Escape Plan" worksheet helps you prepare:

5 Steps to Your Escape Plan

  1. Know your limits. Be clear in your own mind about what you will and will NOT do.
    "When it comes to drugs or alcohol, I refuse to use them or be around people using them. When it comes to shoplifting, I refuse to participate or even be present."
  2. Say how you feel. Don't be embarrassed about expressing discomfort.
    "I believe drugs and alcohol are harmful, and I don't want any part of it. I think shoplifting is wrong and I won't do it."
  3. Have someone to call. Keep their number ready.
    "I can call my parents anytime. I also have my older sibling's number and my best friend's parents' number."
  4. Have a safe place to meet. Know where you can go.
    "Our church, the public library, a family friend's house, or any well-lit public area."
  5. Share your plan with a trusted adult. Get their support and signature.
✚ A Note for Our Family:

Having an escape plan isn't about expecting trouble — it's about being prepared. Joseph literally ran from temptation (Genesis 39), and it was the right thing to do. Sometimes the bravest thing you can do is leave.

"No temptation has overtaken you except what is common to mankind. And God is faithful; he will not let you be tempted beyond what you can bear. But when you are tempted, he will also provide a way out so that you can endure it." — 1 Corinthians 10:13

5. Key Terms

Peer Pressure: Influence from people your own age that can be positive (encouraging healthy choices) or negative (pushing toward risky behavior).
Assertive Communication: Expressing your feelings, needs, and boundaries clearly and respectfully while standing firm.
Passive Response: Going along with something you don't want to do; not expressing your true feelings.
Aggressive Response: Being hostile or threatening in your response; this escalates conflict.
Refusal Skills: Techniques for saying "no" effectively and maintaining your position when pressured.
Escape Plan: A pre-made plan for how to safely leave a risky situation, including who to call and where to go.

6. Worksheet Answers (Lesson Wrap-Up)

Part I — True or False

Statement Answer Why?
Sometimes pressure to do something can actually be good or healthy, like "stay in school," "don't use drugs," and "avoid an unplanned pregnancy." TRUE Not all pressure is bad! Positive peer pressure encourages healthy decisions. Pressure to stay in school, avoid drugs, and prevent pregnancy are all good examples.
Using drugs or alcohol can prevent you from making safe sexual decisions. TRUE Drugs and alcohol impair your judgment, making it much harder to make safe decisions, say no to pressure, or think about consequences.
Ways to avoid a risky sexual situation include using assertive communication to firmly state your limits and expectations. TRUE Assertive communication — clearly and firmly stating your limits — is one of the most effective ways to avoid risky situations and resist pressure.

Part II — What was the most important thing you learned?

"I learned the difference between passive, aggressive, and assertive responses, and that being assertive is the healthiest way to handle pressure. I also learned to have an escape plan ready so I know exactly what to do if I'm ever in a risky situation. Practicing responses to pressure lines ahead of time makes it much easier to say no in the moment."

Part III — How confident are you that you can resist peer and media pressures?

Circle 1 (Not Confident) to 5 (Very Confident). A strong answer is 4 or 5.
