1. The Story: Are We Still Friends?

In the animated video, two friends are talking at their school lockers. One girl invites the other to go shopping after school, but her friend says she can't — she already has plans to hang out with Eva, a new friend.

The first girl is left feeling hurt and replaced. She starts crying because she thinks she's lost her best friend.

The question: Has this ever happened to you? Feeling left out when a friend makes new friends is a very common and normal experience. We'll see how the story ends later in the lesson.

2. Characteristics of a Good Friend

Think about your best friend. What makes them a good friend? Here are key characteristics:

  • Fun — you enjoy spending time together
  • Honesty — they tell you the truth, even when it's hard
  • Trust — you can rely on them and share things with them
  • Helpful — they support you when you need it
  • Forgiving — they don't hold mistakes against you
  • Commitment — they stick with you through good and bad times
  • Flexibility — they can adapt and compromise
  • Shared values — you care about similar things
Important: These same skills that make a great friendship are the same skills needed for any committed long-term relationship, including marriage. Building strong friendships now is practice for healthy relationships later in life.

3. Friendship Inventory (Self-Assessment)

The lesson includes a worksheet where you rate yourself on friendship skills:

  • Step 1: Rate yourself on each characteristic — write "yes," "no," or "sometimes"
  • Step 2: Ask a close friend to rate your friendship skills
  • Step 3: Ask a parent or trusted adult to rate your skills

Characteristics to rate: Closeness, Commitment, Honesty, Communication, Understanding, Empathy, Flexibility, Forgiveness, Fun.

Don't worry if you're not great at all of them! Nobody is perfect at every friendship skill. The point is to see where you're strong and where you can grow. Relationships always take work — and that's okay.

4. Liking vs. Loving

There's an important difference between liking and loving — but they're also very similar!

Things You Might Like

  • Listening to music
  • Watching movies
  • Playing a sport
  • A school subject
  • Going to the beach
  • Hanging out with friends

Things You Might Love

  • Your best friends
  • Your parents and family
  • Your grandparents
  • Your pets
  • A sport or hobby you're passionate about
  • Brothers and sisters

How does liking feel?

  • Fun, interesting, enjoyable
  • You feel happy and engaged
  • You want to learn more or do more of it
  • You smile when you're around it/them

How does loving feel?

  • Similar to liking, but more intense
  • Warm, comforting, exciting
  • You think about this person a lot
  • You choose to be around them whenever you can
  • You want them to be happy

How can you show it?

Showing You Like Someone

  • Smile at them
  • Send a note or card
  • Give a small gift
  • Ask them to be friends
  • Join a club or activity they're in

Showing You Love Someone

  • Give them a hug
  • Tell them "I love you"
  • Do nice things for them (like chores for your parents)
  • Make something for them
  • Make long-term commitments
Key insight: Love is an extension of friendship — a greater, deeper degree of the same thing. The friendship skills you build now (trust, honesty, communication, forgiveness) are the exact same skills that make loving relationships work.

5. Planning Fun Group Activities

Group activities are a great way to build friendship skills. Here are two examples from the lesson:

Example 1: Volleyball at the Park

  • Fun — playing a sport together is enjoyable
  • Communication — you have to work with your team and call out plays
  • Closeness — being on a team creates a sense of unity
  • Flexibility — if someone else gets the ball, you adjust
  • Forgiveness — if someone drops the ball, you move on — everyone makes mistakes

Example 2: Making Cookies for a Sick Friend

  • Communication — working together on the recipe
  • Empathy — sharing stories and understanding each other's feelings
  • Honesty — being open with each other while baking
  • Helpfulness — the whole activity is about helping a friend feel special
Your turn: Think of a fun activity you'd like to do with friends. What friendship skills would you develop by doing it?

6. The Story: How It Ended

In the second half of the video, Dolce finds Sarah crying and asks what's wrong:

  • Sarah says she feels betrayed — "You're hanging out with Eva all the time. What about our friendship?"
  • Dolce apologizes and explains: "I really like Eva a lot. I might even like her more than a friend. But that doesn't mean I still don't really like you as a friend."
  • Sarah listens and responds: "Thank you for telling me. I'm really happy for you and Eva."
  • They both confirm: "Still best friends."
What they did right: They communicated their feelings honestly. Sarah expressed that she felt hurt. Dolce explained what was happening and reassured Sarah. Sarah was able to forgive and be happy for her friend. These are exactly the friendship skills from the lesson in action!

7. Worksheet Answers (Lesson Wrap-Up)

Part I — True or False

StatementAnswerWhy?
Friendship involves trust, honesty, and communication. TRUE These are three of the core characteristics of a good friendship discussed in the lesson.
The skills and characteristics needed for a high-quality friendship are the same skills and characteristics needed in a committed long-term relationship or marriage. TRUE Love is an extension of friendship. The same skills — trust, honesty, communication, forgiveness — are needed for both.
Both liking and loving involve understanding another person's feelings. TRUE Whether you like or love someone, understanding their feelings (empathy) is a key part of the relationship.

Part II — Most important thing you learned?

"I learned that the skills I build in friendships now — like trust, communication, and forgiveness — are the same skills I'll need for any important relationship in the future. I also learned that it's important to communicate when I'm feeling hurt instead of just being sad alone."

Part III — How positive about forming safe and healthy friendships?

Circle 1 (Not Positive) to 5 (Very Positive).

A good answer is 4 or 5. Everyone can build great friendships by practicing these skills!
