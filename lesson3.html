1. The Story: Where Is Your Turban?

In the animated video, a boy named Aman is being picked up by his dad after school. Another student starts bullying him — making fun of his dad's skin color and asking "Where's your turban?" (mocking their Sikh religion).

A second bully laughs along, encouraging the behavior.

This is bullying. Making fun of someone because of their race, religion, appearance, or family is never okay. We'll see how the story ends later.

2. What Is Bullying?

Examples of bullying:

  • Name calling and putting others down
  • Picking on someone's differences
  • Sending rude emails or texts
  • Spreading rumors
  • Posting negative messages about someone online

Why do people bully others?

  • Wanting to control another person — it gives them a feeling of power
  • Personal/family history of assault or abuse — they learned negative behaviors
  • Unable to control emotions and/or behavior
  • Peer pressure — others laughing or encouraging the bully
  • Being uncomfortable about someone else's race, religion, gender, or other differences
Remember: Bullying often continues unless someone takes action to stop it. Doing nothing and hoping it goes away usually doesn't work.

3. Types of Sexual Harassment

  • Verbal harassment: Taunts, name calling, making sexual comments, or saying things like "that is so gay"
  • Physical harassment: Unwanted touches, pinches, pokes, and other physical advances
  • Visual harassment: Posting sexually explicit photos or drawings at school or online
Important: Sexual harassment can result in punishment, suspension from school, or even a lawsuit. It is never okay.

Healthy vs. Unhealthy Relationships

  • Healthy relationships involve: open communication, mutual respect, and consent
  • Unhealthy relationships involve: controlling behavior, harassment, abuse, disrespect

4. Why Consent Is Important

Consent means giving your clear "okay" to an activity. Both people must agree.

A simple example: One friend wants to give a hug. They ask, "Can I give you a hug?" If the other person says "okay," that's consent. If they say "no" or say nothing — no hug. Some people just aren't comfortable with hugs, and that's completely fine.

"Yes Means Yes!"

  • Verbal consent must be given by both people
  • The absence of "no" does NOT mean "yes" — silence is not consent
  • Impaired people cannot give consent — if someone is drunk or high, they cannot truly agree to anything
  • No one should be forced to do anything without their active consent
Bottom line: If someone hasn't clearly said "yes," then the answer is "no." Always respect other people's boundaries, and expect the same respect for yours.

5. Internet Safety

The internet can be dangerous. Some people use it to find and exploit vulnerable teens for illegal activities including pornography, sex trafficking, and kidnapping.

Guidelines for staying safe online:

  • Do NOT visit pornographic websites
  • Do NOT participate in chat rooms where sexual or aggressive behavior is encouraged
  • Do NOT start a relationship with a stranger online
  • Do NOT send or receive nude pictures of yourself or others
What is sexting? Sending nude or sexual photos via text or the internet. Sexting is against the law. You can be labeled as a sex offender for life and be prosecuted. Never do it, and never ask someone else to do it.

6. Protecting Yourself: What Would You Do?

Think about each of these situations. What would you do — do nothing, discourage the behavior, seek help, or end the relationship?

  • Someone makes negative comments about your clothes, body, or hair
  • Someone always decides where you go and what you do, never letting you choose
  • Someone puts you down in public
  • Someone asks you to send nude pictures of yourself
  • Someone offers you alcohol or drugs
  • Someone gets angry when you spend time with others
  • Someone asks you to help them bully another student
  • Someone hits you and then apologizes
  • Someone touches you inappropriately
  • Someone encourages you to join a chat room on a sex-related website
If any of these happen to you: Talk to a parent or trusted adult immediately. Don't try to handle it alone. Think ahead about what you would do, so you're ready if it ever happens.

7. How to Report Sexual Assault

If you or someone you know has been sexually assaulted:

  • Contact your local police
  • Contact a school counselor, school nurse, or social worker
  • Tell a parent or trusted adult

8. The Story: Standing Up for Each Other

In the second half of the video, Aman's friends stand up for him:

  • "My dad wears a turban because of our religion" — Aman proudly explains his Sikh heritage
  • "There are a ton of different religions and ethnicities" — his friends educate the bully
  • "I'm Korean American — learn your geography!" — another friend speaks up
  • The bullies back down
Lessons from the story:
1. Stand up for yourself — be proud of who you are
2. Stand up for others — don't be a bystander
3. Educate — often bullies act out of ignorance. Helping them understand can change their behavior
4. Strength in numbers — friends supporting each other makes a difference

9. Worksheet Answers (Lesson Wrap-Up)

Part I — True or False

StatementAnswerWhy?
If you are being bullied, you should do nothing and hope they stop. FALSE A bully often keeps going unless someone takes action. You should seek help from a parent, teacher, or trusted adult.
Unhealthy relationships involve open communication, mutual respect, and consent. FALSE This is a trick question! Open communication, mutual respect, and consent are characteristics of HEALTHY relationships. Unhealthy relationships lack these qualities.
Saying "that is so gay" is a form of sexual harassment. TRUE Using someone's sexual orientation (or perceived orientation) as an insult is a form of verbal sexual harassment. It's hurtful and inappropriate.

Part II — Most important thing you learned?

"I learned that consent means both people have to clearly say 'yes' — silence is not consent. I also learned that if I'm being bullied or see someone being bullied, I shouldn't do nothing. I should speak up or get help from a trusted adult."

Part III — How positive about protecting yourself from unsafe situations?

Circle 1 (Not Positive) to 5 (Very Positive).

A confident answer is 4 or 5. Now that you know the warning signs and what to do, you're better prepared!
