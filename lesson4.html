1. What Is Human Trafficking?

Human trafficking is a form of modern-day slavery. It involves using force, fraud, or coercion to exploit a person for labor, services, or commercial sex acts. It is a serious crime that happens all over the world — including in the United States.

Key fact: Human trafficking includes both labor trafficking and sex trafficking. It can happen to anyone regardless of age, gender, race, or nationality.

Traffickers prey on vulnerable people — including runaways, homeless youth, foster children, and those who feel isolated or unloved. They use manipulation and deception to control their victims.

✚ A Note for Our Family:

God created every person with inherent dignity and worth. Human trafficking is a grave evil that strips people of their God-given freedom. As Christians, we are called to be aware of injustice and to protect the vulnerable.

"Learn to do right; seek justice. Defend the oppressed. Take up the cause of the fatherless; plead the case of the widow." — Isaiah 1:17

2. Types of Human Trafficking

Labor Trafficking

  • Forcing someone to work without proper pay or under threat
  • Can happen in farms, restaurants, factories, or homes (domestic servitude)
  • Victims are often kept through threats, debt bondage, or confiscation of documents

Sex Trafficking

  • Forcing, coercing, or deceiving someone into commercial sexual acts
  • Any minor (under 18) involved in commercial sex is automatically a victim of trafficking — no force or coercion needs to be proven
  • Traffickers may use online platforms and social media to recruit victims
Remember: Both types are equally serious crimes. Victims are never at fault — the trafficker is the criminal.

3. Warning Signs & How Traffickers Operate

Child sex traffickers often "befriend" their victims by acting like their boyfriend, girlfriend, or someone who really cares about them. This is a deliberate manipulation tactic.

Common tactics traffickers use:

  • Love bombing — showering attention, gifts, and affection to build trust
  • Isolation — separating victims from family and friends
  • Threats — threatening harm to the victim or their loved ones
  • Debt bondage — claiming the victim "owes" them money
  • Online recruitment — using social media, gaming platforms, or messaging apps to target young people
Warning signs someone may be trafficked:
  • Sudden change in behavior or school attendance
  • Having expensive things they can't explain (new phone, clothes, money)
  • An older "boyfriend" or "girlfriend" who is controlling
  • Being secretive about where they go or who they're with
  • Signs of physical abuse or extreme fearfulness
  • Being withdrawn from family and old friends
✚ A Note for Our Family:

Be aware of people who approach with false kindness and hidden motives. The Bible warns us to be wise and discerning about the intentions of others.

"Be wise as serpents and innocent as doves." — Matthew 10:16

4. How to Protect Yourself

  • Stay connected with your family and trusted adults — isolation makes you vulnerable
  • Be careful online — don't share personal information with strangers
  • Trust your instincts — if something feels wrong, it probably is
  • Know the signs — if someone is giving you excessive gifts or attention for no reason, be cautious
  • Never meet an online stranger alone — always tell a parent or trusted adult
  • Have a safety plan — know who to call and where to go if you feel unsafe
Remember: Real love and friendship don't require you to keep secrets from your family or do things that make you uncomfortable.

5. Getting Help & Reporting

If you or someone you know may be a victim of trafficking, there are people who can help:

  • National Runaway Safeline: 1-800-RUNAWAY (1-800-786-2929)
  • National Human Trafficking Hotline: 1-888-373-7888
  • Text "HELP" to 233733 (BeFree)
  • Call 911 if someone is in immediate danger
  • Tell a trusted adult — parent, teacher, school counselor, or law enforcement officer
You are never in trouble for reporting. Reporting is an act of courage that can save someone's life. Even if you're not sure, it's better to report and let professionals investigate.
✚ A Note for Our Family:

Speaking up for those who cannot speak for themselves is one of the most important things we can do as followers of Christ. If you see something wrong, saying something is not "snitching" — it's love in action.

"Speak up for those who cannot speak for themselves, for the rights of all who are destitute." — Proverbs 31:8

6. Key Terms

Human Trafficking: The use of force, fraud, or coercion to exploit a person for labor or commercial sex acts.
Labor Trafficking: Forcing someone to work through threats, violence, or manipulation.
Sex Trafficking: Forcing, coercing, or deceiving someone into commercial sexual acts. For minors, any commercial sex act qualifies.
Coercion: Using threats, intimidation, or psychological manipulation to control someone.
Debt Bondage: Controlling a person by claiming they owe a debt that must be "worked off."
Grooming: The process of building trust and emotional connection with a victim in order to exploit them.
