1. What Is Abstinence?

Abstinence means choosing not to have sexual contact — including vaginal, oral, anal, or digital (hand) contact. Think of abstinence as a verb — it's something you actively do, not just something you avoid.

The most important fact: Sexual abstinence is the only 100% effective way to prevent pregnancy and STIs/HIV. No other method is completely fail-proof.
✚ A Note for Our Family:

As a Christian family, we believe that sexual intimacy is a gift from God designed for marriage. Choosing abstinence isn't just about avoiding consequences — it's about honoring God's plan for our bodies and relationships.

"Flee from sexual immorality. Every other sin a person commits is outside the body, but the sexually immoral person sins against his own body." — 1 Corinthians 6:18

2. Why Choose Abstinence?

There are many good reasons to choose abstinence:

  • 100% effective at preventing pregnancy and STIs when practiced consistently
  • No health risks or side effects unlike some birth control methods
  • Emotional protection — avoids the emotional complications that can come with sexual activity at a young age
  • Aligns with your values — respects your family's values and religious beliefs
  • Focus on your future — allows you to concentrate on school, friendships, and your goals
  • No regrets — you can always choose to become sexually active later, but you can't undo it
Remember: Choosing abstinence is a strong, mature decision. It takes more courage to say "no" than to go along with pressure.

3. How Do You "Do" Abstinence?

The worksheet asks you to think of abstinence as a verb — an active choice you make every day. Here's how:

Step 1: Define it for yourself

"Being sexually abstinent means choosing not to engage in any form of sexual contact (vaginal, oral, anal, or digital) with another person. It means setting clear boundaries and sticking to them."

Step 2: Know your reasons

"Reasons I want to be sexually abstinent: I want to focus on school and my future goals. I want to follow my family's values and my faith. I don't want to risk pregnancy or STIs. I want to wait for the right person and the right time (marriage)."

Step 3: Activities that support abstinence

"Safe, healthy activities I can do with someone without sexual contact: going to the movies, studying together, playing sports or video games, eating out, going to church events, group hangouts with friends, texting and calling, going to school events and dances."

4. Birth Control Methods Overview

While abstinence is the best choice for teens, the lesson also teaches about birth control methods so you understand how they work. This is important knowledge for your future.

Important: No birth control method (other than abstinence) is 100% effective. And most birth control methods do NOT protect against STIs — only condoms provide some STI protection.

Emergency Contraception (EC)

EC is NOT regular birth control — it's for emergencies only (such as if a condom breaks). It must be taken within 3-5 days after unprotected sex. It prevents about 55-85% of predicted pregnancies.

✚ A Note for Our Family:

We believe the best plan is to wait until marriage for sexual intimacy. Learning about birth control doesn't mean we're expected to use it now — it means we're becoming informed about how the body works. Knowledge is not the same as permission. When you're married someday, this knowledge will help you make responsible family-planning decisions together with your spouse.

"The heart of the discerning acquires knowledge, for the ears of the wise seek it out." — Proverbs 18:15

5. Reproductive Health Services

In many states, teens can access free or low-cost reproductive health services, including:

  • STI testing and treatment
  • Pregnancy testing
  • Counseling and education
  • Birth control information and services

Planning if and when to have children is an important part of planning your life. This is something to think about as you grow and set goals for your future.

6. Key Terms

Abstinence: Choosing not to have any sexual contact (vaginal, oral, anal, or digital). The only 100% effective way to prevent pregnancy and STIs.
Contraception / Birth Control: Methods used to prevent pregnancy. Does not necessarily prevent STIs.
Condom: A barrier method (male or female) that also provides some protection against STIs. Must be used correctly every time.
Hormonal Methods: Birth control that uses hormones (estrogen/progestin) to prevent pregnancy — includes the pill, patch, ring, shot, implant, and hormonal IUD.
Emergency Contraception (EC): Medication taken after unprotected sex to help prevent pregnancy. Not intended as regular birth control.
Family Planning: Deciding if and when to have children, and using methods to achieve those goals.
