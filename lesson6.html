1. The Decision-Making Model

When facing an important decision, a structured approach helps you think clearly instead of acting on impulse. The lesson teaches a 6-step decision-making model:

The 6 Steps to Making a Healthy Decision

  1. Define the Problem — What is the decision you need to make? Be specific.
  2. List Possible Options — Brainstorm all the choices you have.
  3. Evaluate Each Option — Consider the pros and cons of each choice.
  4. List Resources — Who can help? What information do you need?
  5. Make the Decision — Choose the best option based on your evaluation.
  6. Act on It, Take Responsibility, and Re-examine — Follow through, own your choice, and check if it was the right one.
Why this matters: Many teens make decisions based on peer pressure or emotions. Having a clear model helps you slow down, think it through, and make choices that align with your values.
✚ A Note for Our Family:

God gives us wisdom to make good decisions. When we face difficult choices, we can pray for guidance, consult His Word, and seek advice from trusted people in our lives.

"If any of you lacks wisdom, you should ask God, who gives generously to all without finding fault, and it will be given to you." — James 1:5

2. Applying the Model: Teen Pregnancy

The worksheet uses the question "When is a person or couple ready to have children?" to practice the decision-making model. Here's how the steps apply:

Step 1: Define the Problem

"When is a person or couple ready to have a child? This is one of the biggest decisions in life and affects everything — education, career, finances, and relationships."

Step 2: List Possible Options

  • While in high school
  • After high school, with a minimum wage job
  • During college, with a part-time job
  • After college, with a higher-paying job
  • Some people may choose not to have children

Step 3: Evaluate Each Option

"In high school: Very difficult — no income, no education yet, can't even drive to doctor appointments. After high school with minimum wage: Extremely tight finances, hard to provide for a child on minimum wage. During college: Would be very stressful juggling classes and parenting. After college with a career: Most stable — education done, steady income, more maturity. Not having children: Also a valid personal choice."

Step 4-5: Resources & Decision

"Resources include: parents, school counselor, financial advisors, healthcare providers. The best decision for most people is to wait until they have completed their education, have a stable career, and are in a committed relationship (marriage) before having children."
✚ A Note for Our Family:

Children are a blessing from God, and bringing them into the world is a sacred responsibility. Being prepared — emotionally, financially, and spiritually — honors both the child and God's design for family.

"Children are a heritage from the Lord, offspring a reward from him." — Psalm 127:3

3. Alternatives for Raising a Child

The lesson discusses three alternatives when someone is facing an unplanned pregnancy:

  • Safe Surrender — California's Safely Surrendered Baby Law allows a parent to surrender a newborn (within 72 hours of birth) at a hospital, fire station, or other designated location with no questions asked.
  • Adoption — Placing the child with another family who will raise them. This can be a loving choice for both the birth parent and the child.
  • Abortion — Terminating the pregnancy. This is a legal option, though it carries significant emotional, ethical, and medical considerations.
The point of this lesson: Understanding these alternatives helps people make informed decisions. The most important takeaway is that prevention through abstinence is the best strategy.

4. Seeking Advice & Resources

When making important decisions, it is critical to seek advice from your parents or trusted adults. They have experience and wisdom to share.

  • Parents and family members
  • School counselors
  • Doctors and healthcare providers
  • Pastors or church mentors
  • Trusted teachers
Key reminder: Don't try to make big decisions alone. The people who care about you want to help. Asking for advice is a sign of maturity, not weakness.

5. Key Terms

Decision-Making Model: A structured approach to making choices by defining the problem, listing options, evaluating pros/cons, identifying resources, deciding, and following through.
Safe Surrender: A law allowing parents to leave a newborn at a designated safe location (like a hospital or fire station) within 72 hours of birth, with no questions asked.
Adoption: The legal process of placing a child with another family to be raised by them.
Pros and Cons: The advantages and disadvantages of each option in a decision.

6. Worksheet Answers (Lesson Wrap-Up)

Part I — True or False

Statement Answer Why?
Three alternatives to raising a child include safe surrender, adoption, and abortion. TRUE These are the three alternatives discussed in the lesson for someone facing an unplanned pregnancy who may not be able to raise the child.
When making an important decision, it is critical to explore your options, and consider the pros and cons of each option. TRUE This is Step 2 and Step 3 of the decision-making model. Evaluating all options and their consequences is essential for good decisions.
When making an important decision, it is not important to seek advice from your parent(s) or trusted adults. FALSE The opposite is true! Seeking advice from parents and trusted adults is very important when making big decisions. They have experience and wisdom to help guide you.

Part II — What was the most important thing you learned?

Write what YOU think was most important. Sample answer:

"I learned a 6-step decision-making model that I can use for any important decision in my life. I also learned that it's really important to think about the pros and cons of each option and to talk to trusted adults before making big choices. Having a structured approach to decisions helps me not act on impulse or peer pressure."

Part III — How positive are you that you will use the decision-making model?

Circle 1 (Not Positive) to 5 (Very Positive).

A strong answer is 4 or 5. Having a reliable process for making decisions is one of the most valuable life skills you can develop.
