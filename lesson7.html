1. What Is Consent?

Consent means giving clear, voluntary, informed permission for something to happen. When it comes to physical or sexual contact, consent must be:

  • Freely given — not forced, pressured, or manipulated
  • Reversible — you can change your mind at any time
  • Informed — you understand what you're agreeing to
  • Enthusiastic — you actually want to (not just "going along with it")
  • Specific — saying yes to one thing doesn't mean yes to everything
Remember: "No" means no. Silence is not consent. Being drunk or high means a person CANNOT give consent. And someone under the age of consent (varies by state) cannot legally give consent.

2. Setting Personal Boundaries

Personal boundaries are the limits you set for yourself about what you're comfortable with. They apply to physical touch, emotional space, online interactions, and more.

Types of boundaries:

  • Physical boundaries — who can touch you, how, and where
  • Emotional boundaries — how much personal information you share
  • Digital boundaries — what you share online, who you talk to, what images you allow
  • Time boundaries — how you spend your time and who you spend it with
Healthy boundaries look like: "I'm not comfortable with that." "Please don't do that." "I need some space right now." These are all perfectly okay things to say.
✚ A Note for Our Family:

God calls us to respect others and ourselves. Setting boundaries is not being rude — it's being wise. Your body is a temple, and you have every right to protect it.

"Do you not know that your bodies are temples of the Holy Spirit, who is in you, whom you have received from God?" — 1 Corinthians 6:19

3. Consent and the Law

There are important legal aspects of consent that everyone should know:

  • Age of consent varies by state (in California, it is 18)
  • Sexual activity with someone under the age of consent is illegal, even if they "agree"
  • Sexual assault is any sexual contact without consent — it is always a crime
  • Sending or receiving sexual images of minors is illegal (even of yourself) — this is considered child pornography
Sexting warning: Sending, receiving, or sharing sexual images of anyone under 18 is a serious crime — even if it's a picture of yourself. It can result in criminal charges and a permanent record.

4. Communicating Boundaries

It can feel awkward to set boundaries, but it's one of the most important skills you can develop:

  • Be clear and direct — say what you mean without being vague
  • Use "I" statements — "I'm not comfortable with..." instead of "You're being weird"
  • Don't apologize for having boundaries — you don't need to justify yourself
  • Walk away if needed — if someone doesn't respect your boundaries, remove yourself from the situation
  • Tell a trusted adult — if someone repeatedly crosses your boundaries, get help
A person who truly respects you will respect your boundaries. Anyone who pressures you, guilts you, or ignores your "no" is not showing you real respect or love.
✚ A Note for Our Family:

True love — the kind God models for us — never forces or manipulates. It is patient and kind. If someone truly cares about you, they will respect your boundaries without question.

"Love is patient, love is kind. It does not dishonor others, it is not self-seeking." — 1 Corinthians 13:4-5

5. Key Terms

Consent: Clear, voluntary, informed, and enthusiastic permission for something to happen. Can be withdrawn at any time.
Personal Boundaries: The limits a person sets regarding their physical, emotional, and digital space.
Age of Consent: The legal age at which a person can agree to sexual activity (18 in California).
Sexual Assault: Any sexual contact that occurs without consent. It is a crime.
Coercion: Using pressure, threats, or manipulation to get someone to do something they don't want to do.
Sexting: Sending sexual images via text/messaging. Illegal for minors and can have serious legal consequences.

6. Worksheet Answers (Lesson Wrap-Up)

Part I — True or False

Statement Answer Why?
Being infected with HIV can cause emotional, social, and physical problems. TRUE HIV affects the whole person — not just physically (weakened immune system) but also emotionally (stress, anxiety, depression) and socially (stigma, relationship challenges).
Families facing a serious medical problem may need help and support from their friends and neighbors. TRUE Serious illnesses like HIV affect the whole family. Community support — from friends, neighbors, church, and support groups — is essential for families dealing with medical challenges.
If a person is infected with HIV, it is always his or her own fault. FALSE Many people get HIV through no fault of their own — babies born to infected mothers, people who received contaminated blood transfusions, or partners who didn't know their spouse was infected. Blaming someone for an illness is wrong.

Part II — What was the most important thing you learned?

Sample answer:

"I learned that consent has to be clear and enthusiastic — not just the absence of 'no.' I also learned that consent can be taken back at any time, and that anyone who truly respects you will always respect your boundaries. I think the most important thing is knowing that setting boundaries is not rude — it's actually a sign of self-respect."

Part III — How committed are you to showing compassion for people with a serious illness including HIV?

Circle 1 (Not Committed) to 5 (Very Committed). A strong answer is 5 (Very Committed). Showing compassion is central to our faith and our humanity.
