1. Signs of a Healthy Relationship

Whether it's a friendship, dating relationship, or family relationship, healthy relationships share common traits:

  • Mutual respect — you value each other's opinions, boundaries, and individuality
  • Trust — you can rely on each other and don't constantly worry or check up
  • Honesty — you communicate openly and truthfully
  • Support — you encourage each other's goals and cheer each other on
  • Equality — both people have equal say; no one dominates
  • Individuality — you maintain your own friends, hobbies, and identity
  • Good communication — you can talk about problems without yelling or shutting down
  • Feeling safe — you feel physically and emotionally safe with the other person
A healthy relationship makes you feel good about yourself. It lifts you up, supports your goals, and respects who you are.
✚ A Note for Our Family:

The Bible gives us a beautiful description of what real love looks like — and it perfectly describes a healthy relationship. Use this as your standard for every relationship in your life.

"Love is patient, love is kind. It does not envy, it does not boast, it is not proud. It does not dishonor others, it is not self-seeking, it is not easily angered, it keeps no record of wrongs." — 1 Corinthians 13:4-5

2. Signs of an Unhealthy Relationship

Warning Signs

  • Controlling behavior
  • Extreme jealousy
  • Isolating you from friends/family
  • Constant put-downs
  • Checking your phone/messages
  • Pressuring you to do things

Red Flags (Get Help)

  • Any physical violence
  • Threats of harm
  • Forcing sexual contact
  • Destroying your property
  • Threatening to hurt themselves if you leave
  • Making you feel afraid
If you or someone you know is in an abusive relationship:
  • National Dating Abuse Hotline: 1-866-331-9474
  • Text "LOVEIS" to 22522
  • Talk to a parent, school counselor, or trusted adult
  • Call 911 if you are in immediate danger

3. Healthy Communication

Good communication is the foundation of every healthy relationship. Here are the key skills:

  • Active listening — pay attention, make eye contact, don't interrupt
  • "I" statements — say "I feel hurt when..." instead of "You always..."
  • Respect differences — it's okay to disagree; how you handle disagreement matters
  • Stay calm — take a break if emotions get too heated; come back to discuss later
  • Be honest but kind — truth doesn't have to be delivered harshly
Communication tip: "I feel [emotion] when [specific situation] because [reason]. I would like [what you want instead]." This formula helps you express yourself clearly without attacking the other person.

4. Dating Safety

  • Tell a parent where you're going, who you're with, and when you'll be back
  • Group dates are safer and more fun, especially when you're younger
  • Meet in public places for first dates or meetups
  • Have your own transportation or a ride you can call
  • Trust your gut — if something feels off, leave
  • Keep your phone charged and accessible
✚ A Note for Our Family:

The Bible teaches us to choose our companions wisely. The people we surround ourselves with shape who we become. Look for friends and future relationships that help you grow closer to God, not further away.

"Walk with the wise and become wise, for a companion of fools suffers harm." — Proverbs 13:20

5. Key Terms

Healthy Relationship: A relationship built on mutual respect, trust, honesty, support, equality, and good communication.
Unhealthy Relationship: A relationship characterized by control, jealousy, manipulation, disrespect, or abuse.
Active Listening: Fully concentrating on what someone is saying, showing you understand, and responding thoughtfully.
"I" Statements: A communication technique that expresses feelings without blaming (e.g., "I feel..." instead of "You always...").
Dating Violence: Physical, emotional, or sexual abuse within a dating relationship. It is never acceptable.

6. Worksheet Answers (Lesson Wrap-Up)

Part I — True or False

Statement Answer Why?
The Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV) prevents the body from being able to fight off infections. TRUE HIV attacks the CD4 (T) cells of the immune system. Without these cells, the body cannot effectively fight infections, leading eventually to AIDS.
Body fluids which can spread HIV include blood, semen, vaginal fluids, and urine. FALSE Blood, semen, vaginal fluids, breast milk, and rectal fluids can spread HIV. However, urine cannot spread HIV. Neither can sweat, tears, or saliva.
The goal of antiretroviral therapy (ART) is to extend the infected person's life by keeping his or her HIV viral load low. TRUE ART works by keeping the viral load as low as possible (ideally "undetectable"). This protects the immune system and allows the person to live a long, healthy life.
